Trauma Taxonomy & Sources

What is Trauma Recovery

The gradual restoration of safety, flexibility, agency, and connection in a nervous system that has learned to survive without them.

Primary Goal
Restore Nervous System Safety
Reduce hypervigilance and collapse states through predictability, rhythm, and containment.
Primary Goal
Rebuild Circadian Rhythm
Anchor sleep/wake times. Morning sunlight. Consistent mealtimes. Reduce light disruption.
Primary Goal
Repair Metabolic & Inflammatory Damage
Address cortisol and glucose variability, micronutrient depletion, neuroinflammation, and sleep architecture.
Primary Goal
Re-establish Agency, Meaning & Embodiment
Choice in pacing. No "shoulds." Restoration of agency is non-negotiable.
What Recovery Is Not

Not designing avoidance. Planning for damage control and preservation in environments that inevitably contain:

Uncertainty
Recovery builds tolerance for ambiguity, not elimination of it.
Threat
The goal is resilience and rapid recovery — not a threat-free life.
Moral Strain
Practice to strengthen, integrate, and deepen under pressure.
Cognitive Overload
Building capacity to function well without burning out.
Deeper Healing Arc

Beyond routine, healing comes through:

Reclaiming agency
Acting from choice rather than survival.
Restoring dignity
Rebuilding a self that is worthy of care.
Creating beauty
Therapeutic creation as healing, not distraction.
Belonging again
Safe, titrated re-engagement with others.
Building a future narrative
A life oriented forward, not organised around survival.

Core Principles

The foundations that guide every decision in recovery. Skills so far — each with its theme, what it prioritises, and why it matters.

1
Safety Before Insight
Theme: The nervous system must feel safe before it can change.

Trauma is not stored as a story — it's stored as threat physiology. No amount of cognitive understanding helps if the body still believes danger is present.

What this prioritises
  • Predictability, rhythm, and containment
  • Reduction of sudden demands, novelty, or pressure
  • Gentle pacing over intensity
Why it matters

Without felt safety: learning shuts down · memory reconsolidation fails · insight becomes destabilising. Safety is not comfort — it's absence of threat.

2
Bottom-Up Regulation
Theme: The body leads; the mind follows.

Trauma dysregulates: vagal tone · breath patterns · muscle tone · gut-brain signalling.

What this prioritises
  • Breath, posture, movement, temperature, rhythm
  • Interoception (noticing internal states without judgment)
  • Somatic tracking without forcing release
Why it matters

Top-down approaches alone can: increase dissociation · reinforce self-monitoring and control · retraumatise through premature processing. Healing happens when the body updates first.

3
Capacity Building, Not Catharsis
Theme: Expand capacity before touching content.

Old trauma models emphasised emotional discharge. Modern neuroscience shows that capacity — not expression — predicts recovery.

What this prioritises
  • Widening the window of tolerance
  • Micro-dosing activation and rest
  • Resourcing before exposure
Why it matters

Without capacity: emotional flooding occurs · shutdown or collapse follows · symptoms worsen even if "insight" increases. You don't heal trauma by feeling more — you heal it by being able to feel safely.

4
Nervous System Flexibility Over Calm
Theme: The goal is adaptability, not constant calm.

A regulated system is not always relaxed — it's responsive and resilient.

What this prioritises
  • Smooth transitions between states
  • Ability to mobilise and settle
  • Recovery speed after stress
Why it matters

Trauma locks the system into: chronic hyperarousal · chronic collapse · rigid oscillation between the two. Healing restores choice.

5
Integration, Not Erasure
Theme: Trauma becomes part of the story, not the whole story.

Recovery doesn't mean forgetting or "getting over it." It means: memory loses its charge · identity expands beyond survival roles · past no longer hijacks present perception.

What this prioritises
  • Meaning-making after regulation
  • Narrative coherence
  • Gentle identity reorganisation
Why it matters

Trying to eliminate trauma memories often: increases avoidance · reinforces fear · maintains fragmentation. Integration brings continuity.

6
Energy & Resource Restoration
Theme: Trauma is metabolically expensive.

Chronic stress depletes: glucose regulation · mitochondrial function · micronutrients · sleep architecture.

What this prioritises
  • Nutritional repletion
  • Rest without guilt
  • Reducing decision fatigue
  • Stabilising circadian rhythms
Why it matters

A depleted system cannot heal — it can only cope. Food, sleep, light exposure, and pacing are therapeutic, not ancillary.

7
Relational Repair
Theme: Trauma happens in relationship; healing completes there.

Even developmental or attachment trauma affects: threat detection · trust calibration · co-regulation capacity.

What this prioritises
  • Safe, bounded connection
  • Internal reparenting / parts integration
  • Gradual re-engagement with others
Why it matters

Isolation protects initially — but prolongs trauma long-term. Connection must be optional, titrated, and safe.

8
Choice, Agency & Consent
Theme: Restoration of agency is non-negotiable.

Trauma is fundamentally about loss of control.

What this prioritises
  • Choice in pacing
  • Opt-out options
  • Collaborative decision-making
  • No "shoulds"
Why it matters

Even well-intentioned protocols can retraumatise if they: override signals · rush readiness · impose timelines. Agency is the medicine.

9
Nonlinear Progress & Cycles
Theme: Healing spirals, it doesn't ascend in a straight line.

Expect: waves · plateaus · regressions that are actually integration phases.

What this prioritises
  • Normalising fluctuation
  • Tracking trends, not days
  • Compassion for pauses
Why it matters

Misinterpreting integration phases as "failure" is one of the most common derailments.

10
Gentleness Is a Strategic Choice
Theme: Slower is often faster.

Trauma systems heal through precision, not force.

What this prioritises
  • Low-intensity, high-frequency inputs
  • Consistency over heroics
  • Respect for limits
Why it matters

The nervous system learns safety through repetition, not breakthrough moments.

The nervous system heals in waves, not levels. It is normal to return to Tier 1 tools during Tier 3 phases.

Tier 1 — Stabilise & Repair

Rebuild baseline safety and physiological integrity. Timeline: ~8–16 weeks depending on severity.

Primary Objective
Restore autonomic regulation, metabolic stability, and cognitive baseline.
Core Themes
SafetyPredictabilityCapacity buildingNervous system containment
1
Nervous System Regulation
  • Daily vagal toning (slow nasal breathing, extended exhales)
  • Gentle somatic tracking (without trauma processing)
  • Consistent sleep/wake schedule
  • HRV monitoring (trend tracking, not obsession)
Goal: Reduce hypervigilance spikes and collapse states.
2
Metabolic & Neurochemical Repair
  • Anti-inflammatory keto or low-glycemic protocol
  • Omega-3 repletion
  • Magnesium glycinate
  • Glycine before bed
  • Stable protein intake (1.6–2.2g/kg)
Goal: Stabilise cortisol and glucose variability.
3
Cognitive Baseline Restoration
  • Light working memory drills (5–10 min)
  • Reading with recall summaries
  • Focus intervals (25 min max initially)
Goal: Restore sustained attention without overload.
4
Environmental Stabilisation
  • Fixed workspace
  • Predictable lighting
  • Reduced digital chaos
  • Evening light hygiene
5
Load Management
  • Strict energy budgeting
  • No high-intensity stress training yet
  • Clear work/rest boundaries
Exit Criteria for Tier 1
  • Sleep consistent 80% of nights
  • Reduced startle reactivity
  • Emotional spikes shorter in duration
  • No frequent cognitive crashes
Timeline: ~8–16 weeks depending on severity.

Tier 2 — Strengthen & Expand

Increase capacity and controlled stress tolerance. Timeline: 3–6 months.

Primary Objective
Build resilience, expand window of tolerance, improve cognitive stamina.
Entry Requirement
All Tier 1 exit criteria met. Do not begin Tier 2 without a stable autonomic baseline.
1
Autonomic Agility Training
  • Controlled cold exposure (brief)
  • Moderate-intensity strength training
  • Breath-led state shifting (calm → alert → calm)
  • Stress inoculation drills (mild, reversible)
Goal: Improve recovery speed after activation.
2
Cognitive Load Expansion
  • Dual-task training
  • Memory palaces
  • Timed analytical problem solving
  • Bias detection training
Goal: Increase executive function under mild stress.
3
Emotional Range Training
  • Emotional granularity exercises
  • Attachment recalibration work
  • Boundary setting drills
  • Interpersonal exposure in low-risk contexts
Goal: Reduce threat bias in social processing.
4
Hormonal Optimisation
  • Cortisol curve testing (if available)
  • Testosterone/estrogen balance assessment
  • Optimise sleep depth (tracking deep sleep %)
5
Embodied Strength
  • Progressive overload training
  • Sprint intervals (carefully dosed)
  • Martial arts or strength training (optional)
Goal: Rebuild physical agency.
Exit Criteria for Tier 2
  • Fast return to baseline after stress
  • Can tolerate moderate cognitive pressure
  • Improved HRV recovery curve
  • Emotional range without collapse
Timeline: 3–6 months.

Tier 3 — Integrate & Optimise

Cognitive and emotional flexibility under operational load. Timeline: 6–18 months cumulative.

Primary Objective
Train performance without destabilising trauma repair.
Critical Warning
It is normal to temporarily return to Tier 1 tools during Tier 3 phases. The nervous system heals in waves, not levels.
1
High-Load Cognitive Simulation
  • Scenario simulations
  • Ambiguity tolerance training
  • Red-team/blue-team exercises for my context
  • Moral complexity drills
Goal: Enhance decision-making under pressure.
2
State Architecture Mastery
  • Intentional state shifting within 60 seconds
  • Alert focus on command
  • Controlled sympathetic activation for tasks
  • Rapid parasympathetic recovery
3
Advanced Interventions (Optional)
  • Neurofeedback
  • EMDR (only if Tier 1 capacity is stable)
  • Psychedelic-assisted therapy (legal & supervised only)
  • Precision supplementation (creatine, acetyl-L-carnitine, etc.)
4
Cognitive Armour & Decompression
  • Post-exposure ritual
  • Weekly psychological detox
  • Structured reflection protocol
  • Narrative coherence journalling
Goal: Prevent cumulative cognitive toxicity.
5
Ethical & Identity Integration
  • Values audit
  • Moral injury prevention framework
  • Leadership identity shaping
Exit Criteria for Tier 3
  • Maintains performance under stress without rebound crash
  • No chronic hyperarousal after exposure
  • Stable self-concept
  • Clear ethical orientation
Timeline: 6–18 months cumulative.

Hazards & Recovery

Environments, habits, and patterns that actively damage nervous system recovery — with specific recovery protocols for each.

The Five Greatest Hazards & Protectors
Five Greatest Hazards
  • 1. Sleep disruption
  • 2. Chronic stress / hypervigilance
  • 3. Alcohol
  • 4. Highly processed foods
  • 5. Multitasking
Five Greatest Protectors
  • 1. Sleep quality
  • 2. Aerobic movement
  • 3. Deep focus blocks
  • 4. Omega-3 + choline
  • 5. Safe relationships
Environmental Hazards
1
Chronic Unpredictability
Examples
  • Irregular schedules
  • Frequent travel across time zones
  • Shift work
  • Constant plan changes
Why harmful
  • Disrupts circadian rhythm
  • Impairs hippocampal memory encoding
  • Elevates baseline cortisol
  • Weakens prefrontal control
Recovery if unavoidable
  • Anchor wake/sleep time within ±30 minutes
  • Morning sunlight every day
  • Melatonin only short-term if needed
  • 3–5 days of rhythm stabilisation after disruption
2
High-Threat / High-Noise Environments
Examples
  • Loud cities constantly
  • Crowds
  • Conflict-heavy workplaces
  • Sirens, alarms, construction
Why harmful
  • Keeps amygdala hyperactive
  • Reduces working memory
  • Inhibits hippocampal neurogenesis
Recovery
  • 60–120 min daily in quiet nature
  • Noise-canceling headphones
  • Evening vagal toning
  • White noise at night
3
Emotionally Unsafe Spaces
Examples
  • Hostile teams
  • Unstable leadership
  • Shaming cultures
  • Moral injury environments
Why harmful
  • Suppresses PFC
  • Increases threat bias
  • Hardens trauma circuits
  • Degrades ethical reasoning
Recovery
  • One daily psychologically safe interaction
  • Journalling to reassert narrative coherence
  • Therapy or peer supervision weekly
4
Chronic Multitasking
Why harmful
  • Reduces gray matter in anterior cingulate
  • Degrades working memory
  • Impairs error detection
  • Trains distractibility
Avoid
  • Task switching
  • Multiple tabs
  • Notifications during deep work
Recovery
  • 2–3 weeks of strict monotasking
  • Daily 90-minute deep work block
  • Reaction-time retraining
5
Prolonged Hypervigilance / Threat Monitoring
Examples
  • Constant news
  • Monitoring conflicts
  • Scanning for threats
Why harmful
  • Keeps amygdala dominant
  • Shrinks hippocampus
  • Reduces abstraction ability
  • Promotes rigid thinking
Recovery
  • News fast (3–14 days)
  • Daily nature horizon scanning
  • Trauma-informed therapy
  • REM-sleep prioritisation
6
Overanalysis of Trauma Content
Why harmful
  • Reinforces fear networks
  • Strengthens maladaptive memory traces
  • Inhibits extinction learning
Recovery
  • Contain trauma work to therapy windows
  • Somatic discharge after sessions
  • Positive memory induction before sleep
7
Excessive HIIT / Overtraining
Why harmful
  • Suppresses BDNF long-term
  • Increases neuroinflammation
  • Impairs sleep
  • Depletes dopamine
Avoid
  • Daily HIIT
  • Training to exhaustion
  • Chronic soreness
Recovery
  • 2–3 weeks zone-2 only
  • Magnesium + glycine
  • HRV-guided training
8
Training While Sleep-Deprived
Why harmful
  • Prevents myelin repair
  • Impairs motor learning
  • Increases injury & neurodegeneration risk
Recovery
  • Skip intensity
  • Nap + NSDR
  • Prioritise sleep that night

Behaviour as Medicine

Tailoring recovery to your specific nervous system. Every intervention is a variable to test — not a rule to follow.

Guidelines as I Experiment
Rule 1
70% Rule

Always stop with energy left. Never push to depletion — the nervous system learns safety from the experience of having reserves, not from exhausting them.

Rule 2
1:1 Output to Restoration Ratio

For every hour of effort → one hour of body-based restoration somewhere in the day. Cognitive work counts as load. Restoration means somatic, not passive consumption.

Rule 3
Daily Anchors

Every day must include:

  • Nature
  • Movement
  • Warmth
  • One meaningful act
  • One relationship moment

Options That Could Help

Nine domains to explore. For each, the question is not "should I do this?" but "what does my nervous system actually respond to?"

when? (temporal)

Timing of inputs matters as much as the input itself. When do I train? When do I eat? When do I do cognitive work? When do I rest?

Explore
  • Morning vs evening exercise
  • Eating window alignment with energy
  • Deep work block timing
  • When social interaction feels safe vs depleting
where? (environmental)

Environment is not backdrop — it is input. What spaces signal safety? What spaces activate threat?

Explore
  • Indoor vs outdoor work
  • Lighting conditions
  • Noise levels
  • Nature proximity
  • Temperature of workspace
how much? (load)

The same activity at different doses produces different results. More is rarely better during recovery.

Explore
  • Optimal session length before diminishing returns
  • Number of social interactions per day
  • Volume of cognitive work
  • Exercise intensity and frequency
what state? (autonomic)

Which state am I in when I do things? The same activity in a regulated vs dysregulated state produces entirely different outcomes.

Explore
  • Pre-task regulation rituals
  • State-checking before difficult activities
  • Transition practices between modes
  • What reliably shifts my state
what fuel? (metabolic)

Food, fasting, hydration, and supplementation as variables. How does my nervous system respond to different metabolic inputs?

Explore
  • Meal timing and cognitive performance
  • Protein at breakfast and mood stability
  • Caffeine timing and anxiety
  • Fasting tolerance
  • Which supplements noticeably shift how I feel
what body? (physical)

Which physical practices actually change my nervous system state — vs which feel productive but don't?

Explore
  • Type of exercise and mood effect
  • Cold exposure response
  • Breath work that actually helps
  • Posture and state connection
  • Touch and warmth as regulation
what thinking? (cognitive)

Which cognitive practices build capacity vs which increase rigidity or rumination?

Explore
  • Journalling style that helps vs spirals
  • Reading vs active learning
  • Planning intensity
  • When reflection is useful vs ruminative
what emotions? (emotional)

Which emotional practices expand capacity vs which dysregulate or exhaust?

Explore
  • How much emotional processing is helpful per day
  • What creative outlets discharge vs amplify
  • Which relationships are regulating vs activating
  • What helps after difficult emotional experiences
who am I? (identity)

Which activities reinforce a stable, expanding sense of self — vs which collapse me into survival identity?

Explore
  • Work that connects to values vs work that feels like performance
  • Contribution that energises vs obligation that depletes
  • Which relationships reflect back who I'm becoming

Self-Audit Tool

Rate each domain independently. Do not average across domains.

How to use this tool

For each statement, rate yourself 0–5. Score each domain separately.

0 = Severely compromised 1 = Frequently unstable 2 = Inconsistent / fragile 3 = Generally stable, not optimised 4 = Strong and reliable 5 = Elite-level, adaptable under stress
Can I shift nervous system states intentionally and recover quickly?
  • I can reliably calm myself within 5–10 minutes when activated.
  • I recover physiologically from stress within hours (not days).
  • My sleep onset is stable and predictable.
  • I do not live in chronic background tension.
  • I can access focused alertness on demand without anxiety.
  • I can shift from work mode to rest mode smoothly.
Score / 30
0–12 → Tier 1 focus required 13–20 → Capacity building needed 21–25 → Solid foundation 26–30 → Operational agility
Is my biology supporting cognition?
  • Energy is stable throughout the day.
  • I do not experience major energy crashes.
  • Inflammation symptoms are minimal (pain, brain fog, fatigue).
  • Sleep feels restorative.
  • I tolerate fasting or cognitive load without irritability.
  • My mood is not strongly tied to food timing.

Scoring Interpretation

Step 1 — Identify my weakest 2 domains

Those are your primary leverage points.

Step 2 — Identify my strongest 2 domains

Those are stabilisers. Use them to support weaker areas.

Step 3 — Determine Tier Placement
  • Mostly 0–2 scores → Tier 1 stabilisation focus
  • Mostly 2–3 scores → Tier 2 capacity building
  • Mostly 3–4 scores → Tier 3 optimisation
  • Mostly 4–5 scores → Tier 4 sustain & refine

I may be Tier 3 cognitively but Tier 1 autonomically. That mismatch is common.

Advanced Insight Layer

After scoring, ask:

  1. Where am I compensating?
  2. Where am I strong but fragile?
  3. Where am I stable even under fatigue?
  4. Which domain feels emotionally charged to look at?

Burnout Screening
Exhaustion signals
  • Persistent fatigue not resolved by rest.
  • Emotional flatness or numbing.

Trauma Taxonomy & Sources to Explore

A guide to the thinkers, frameworks, and works that underpin trauma understanding. Organised by the nine types of trauma taxonomy.

Most significant human suffering rarely fits a single category. These types are not rigid containers — they interact, compound, and layer. The purpose of this taxonomy is to expand the frame within which suffering is understood, and to give language to dimensions of experience that may previously have been unnamed.

Type 1 — By Duration & Pattern
Acute Trauma (Type I)
Lenore Terr
Foundational differentiation of Type I (acute) and Type II (chronic) trauma. Acute trauma produces vivid, intrusive memories — the mind's attempt to process something too fast and too intense to metabolise.
Key work: Too Scared to Cry (1990)
Chronic Trauma (Type II)
Lenore Terr
Chronic trauma produces denial, psychic numbing, dissociation, and persistent personality changes. The nervous system adapts to sustained threat in ways that become deeply ingrained — the sense that safety is no longer possible.
Key work: Too Scared to Cry (1990)
Complex Trauma (C-PTSD)
Judith Herman
Consequences of prolonged, repeated trauma within inescapable relationships. Beyond PTSD: chronic shame, emotional dysregulation, distorted self-beliefs, dissociation. Recognised in ICD-11. Perhaps the most important category for trauma-informed practice.
Key work: Trauma and Recovery (1992)
Cumulative Trauma
Multiple theorists
The accumulation of smaller adverse experiences — none meeting clinical trauma threshold individually — that together overwhelm adaptive capacity. Critical for those who feel deeply damaged but cannot identify a single defining event.
See also: Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACE) research
Type 2 — By Developmental Timing
Developmental Trauma
Bessel van der Kolk
Cumulative impact of adverse childhood experiences on developing neurological, psychological, and social systems. Van der Kolk argued the PTSD framework was inadequate and proposed developmental trauma disorder as a distinct category.
Key work: The Body Keeps the Score (2014)
Early Childhood & Perinatal Trauma
Allan Schore
Early trauma is stored somatically — in the body, in physiological reactivity, in implicit relational knowing — rather than as explicit narrative memory. Schore's regulation theory addresses the neurobiological roots of early relational trauma.
Key work: The Science of the Art of Psychotherapy (2012)
Type 3 — By Relational Context
Relational & Attachment Trauma
John Bowlby · Mary Main · Peter Fonagy
Trauma within caregiving relationships disrupts the capacity to form safe, trusting attachments. Disorganised attachment — where the source of fear is also the source of comfort — is the pattern most strongly associated with attachment trauma.
Key works: Bowlby's Attachment and Loss trilogy; Fonagy's Affect Regulation, Mentalization and the Development of the Self
Betrayal Trauma
Jennifer Freyd
Trauma inflicted by someone on whom the victim depends. The mind suppresses knowledge of betrayal to preserve a necessary attachment. Produces particular injuries to trust, identity, and healthy dependency — often with intense self-blame.
Key work: Betrayal Trauma: The Logic of Forgetting Childhood Abuse (1996)
Vicarious Trauma
McCann & Pearlman
The cumulative transformative effect on helpers and witnesses of empathic engagement with traumatised people. Changes the helper's frame of reference, identity, spirituality, and capacity for trust. Distinct from burnout — it is a moral injury as much as a psychological one.
Key work: Psychological Trauma and the Adult Survivor (1990)
Compassion Fatigue
Charles Figley
The erosion of the helper's capacity for empathy through sustained exposure to others' suffering. Where vicarious trauma emphasises structural changes in the helper's inner world, compassion fatigue emphasises depletion of emotional and motivational resources.
Key work: Compassion Fatigue (1995)
Type 4 — By Source & Context
Combat & Moral Injury
Jonathan Shay · Brett Litz
Combat trauma sits at the centre of trauma theory's development — shell shock, combat fatigue, PTSD were all named in relation to soldiers. Shay and Litz distinguished moral injury (guilt, shame, moral anguish) from fear-based PTSD.
Key works: Shay's Achilles in Vietnam (1994); Litz et al., Moral Injury and Moral Repair in War Veterans (2009)
Medical & Birth Trauma
Multiple researchers
Frightening, painful, or dehumanising medical experiences that disrupt bodily autonomy and safety. Medical trauma often includes helplessness, fear of death, and the experience of the body as dangerous or failing. Birth trauma affects mothers, infants, and partners.
See: Journal of Traumatic Stress; Penny Simkin's work on birth trauma
Refugee & Displacement Trauma
Multiple researchers
Cumulative trauma combining pre-migration harm, the trauma of the journey, and post-migration stressors including poverty, discrimination, legal uncertainty, and social isolation. Both deeply personal and irreducibly political.
See: Derrick Silove's ADAPT model; Richard Mollica's work on refugee mental health
Type 5 — By Social & Structural Location
Racial Trauma
Robert Carter · Resmaa Menakem
Psychological harm caused by racism — direct violence, microaggressions, systemic discrimination, and vicarious exposure to racist violence. Ongoing rather than discrete; systemic rather than merely interpersonal; frequently denied. Menakem's somatic account addresses the embodied, intergenerational transmission of racial trauma.
Key works: Carter's Race-Based Traumatic Stress; Menakem's My Grandmother's Hands (2017)
Historical & Intergenerational Trauma
Maria Yellow Horse Brave Heart
Cumulative psychological and cultural wounds carried across generations by communities that experienced collective atrocities. Growing epigenetic research suggests trauma can be transmitted biologically as well as psychologically and culturally.
Key work: Brave Heart's work on historical trauma in Native American communities; see also epigenetics research by Rachel Yehuda
LGBTQ+ Trauma / Minority Stress
Ilan Meyer
The chronic stress, discrimination, rejection, and internalised stigma experienced by LGBTQ+ people. Meyer's minority stress model distinguishes distal stressors (discrimination, violence) from proximal stressors (internalised homophobia, concealment, expectation of rejection).
Key work: Meyer, Prejudice, Social Stress, and Mental Health in Lesbian, Gay, and Bisexual Populations (2003)
Poverty & Economic Trauma
Multiple researchers
Chronic psychological injury of material deprivation — affecting cognitive functioning, developmental opportunities, health outcomes, and life expectancy. Frequently invisible within frameworks focused on discrete traumatic events.
See: Sendhil Mullainathan & Eldar Shafir, Scarcity (2013); ACE research
Type 6 — By Institutional & Systemic Context
Religious & Spiritual Abuse Trauma
Marlene Winell
Abuse of spiritual authority, coercive control in the name of doctrine, shaming of normal human experience. Winell's concept of religious trauma syndrome describes the particular cluster of symptoms from leaving fundamentalist communities — grief, identity disorientation, loss of community, existential distress.
Key work: Winell, Leaving the Fold (1993)
Institutional Trauma
Multiple researchers
Abuse, neglect, or dehumanisation within institutions — schools, hospitals, care homes, prisons, religious organisations, the military. Characterised by power differentials, normalisation of harmful practices, and the way institutional authority compounds personal harm.
See: Herman's Trauma and Recovery; literature on prison reform and institutional care
Type 7 — By Existential & Meaning Dimensions
Moral Injury
Jonathan Shay · Brett Litz
The psychological wound from participating in, witnessing, or failing to prevent actions that transgress deeply held moral beliefs. Primary experience is not fear but guilt, shame, and moral anguish. Affects soldiers, healthcare workers, social workers, journalists — anyone whose work violates their moral convictions.
Key work: Shay, Achilles in Vietnam (1994); Litz et al., Moral Injury and Moral Repair (2009)
Existential & Shattered Assumptions
Ronnie Janoff-Bulman
Trauma that destroys taken-for-granted beliefs underpinning normal functioning — the world is benevolent, meaningful, the self is worthy. Near-death experiences, sudden catastrophic loss, and encounters with extreme evil can all produce this form of injury.
Key work: Shattered Assumptions: Towards a New Psychology of Trauma (1992)
Spiritual Trauma
Multiple thinkers
Shattering of a person's relationship with the divine or framework of ultimate meaning. Involves the collapse of the framework within which suffering itself was made meaningful. Requires approaches that can hold existential and spiritual dimensions alongside psychological ones.
See: Winell; also works by Thomas Moore and Richard Rohr on spiritual wounding
Type 8 — By Body & Somatic Dimensions
Somatic Trauma
Peter Levine · Bessel van der Kolk
Trauma is fundamentally a disruption of embodied life — stored in the body as chronic tension, autonomic dysregulation, and somatic symptoms. Recovery requires working with the body as well as the mind. May manifest as chronic pain, fatigue, autoimmune conditions, or disconnection from bodily experience.
Key works: Levine, Waking the Tiger (1997); van der Kolk, The Body Keeps the Score (2014)
Interoceptive Trauma
Antonio Damasio · Stephen Porges
Disruption of interoception — the sense of the internal state of the body. Traumatised people show either overwhelming sensitivity to bodily sensations or profound numbing. Because interoception is foundational to emotional awareness and decision-making, its disruption has wide-ranging consequences.
Key works: Damasio, The Feeling of What Happens; Porges, The Polyvagal Theory (2011)
Type 9 — By Relationship to Self
Structural Dissociation
van der Hart · Nijenhuis · Steele
The splitting of the personality into apparently normal parts that carry on daily functioning and emotional parts that hold traumatic experience. Illuminates the experience of feeling divided against oneself in ways that cannot be explained without a trauma framework.
Key work: The Haunted Self: Structural Dissociation and the Treatment of Chronic Traumatization (2006)
Shame-Based Trauma
June Price Tangney · Brené Brown
Trauma in which shame is the primary organising emotion — the deep conviction that the self is fundamentally bad, defective, or unworthy. Shame is totalising: not about what one did but about what one is. Particularly associated with abuse, neglect, and relational trauma.
Key works: Tangney & Dearing, Shame and Guilt (2002); Brown, I Thought It Was Just Me (2007)
Identity Trauma
Multiple theorists
The disruption of a coherent, stable, and positive sense of self by traumatic experience. Consequences include identity confusion, chronic shame, difficulty knowing what one feels or wants, and a pervasive sense of inauthenticity or unreality about the self.
See: Herman; van der Kolk; IFS model (Richard Schwartz, Internal Family Systems Therapy)
